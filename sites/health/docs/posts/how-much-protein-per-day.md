# How Much Protein Do You Need Per Day? A Complete, Evidence-Based Guide

Protein is everywhere in the wellness conversation -- from high-protein snack labels to fitness influencers preaching the gospel of protein shakes. But how much do you actually need? The answer depends on your age, activity level, body composition goals, and overall health.

This guide breaks down the science so you can figure out your ideal protein intake without the guesswork or the hype.

<div class="tldr">

**TL;DR:** The Recommended Dietary Allowance (RDA) for protein is 0.8 grams per kilogram of body weight per day for the average adult, but this is a minimum to prevent deficiency -- not necessarily an optimal amount. Active individuals, older adults, and those aiming for muscle gain or fat loss generally benefit from 1.2-2.0 g/kg/day. Protein should be spread across meals (20-40 g per meal) for best results. Most people can meet their needs through whole foods without supplements.

## What Is Protein and Why Does It Matter?

Protein is one of three macronutrients (along with carbohydrates and fat) and is made up of amino acids -- the building blocks your body uses to repair tissues, produce enzymes and hormones, support immune function, and maintain muscle mass.

Of the 20 amino acids your body uses, 9 are considered "essential," meaning you must get them from food because your body cannot make them. Foods that contain all 9 essential amino acids in adequate proportions are called "complete" protein sources. Most animal proteins are complete, while most plant proteins (with exceptions like soy and quinoa) are incomplete individually but can be combined throughout the day to meet your needs.

## The Baseline: RDA for Protein

The Recommended Dietary Allowance (RDA) established by the National Academy of Medicine is:

**0.8 grams of protein per kilogram of body weight per day (0.36 g per pound)**

For a 70 kg (154 lb) person, that works out to about 56 grams per day.

However, it is important to understand what the RDA actually represents. It is the minimum amount needed to prevent deficiency in 97.5% of the healthy adult population. It was not designed to reflect the optimal intake for active individuals, older adults, or people with specific body composition goals.

Many researchers and professional organizations now recommend higher intakes for various populations, as outlined below.

## Protein Needs by Goal and Activity Level

### Sedentary Adults

**Recommendation: 0.8-1.0 g/kg/day**

If you are not regularly exercising and have no specific fitness goals, the RDA range is generally adequate. However, some nutrition researchers argue that even sedentary individuals may benefit from slightly higher intakes (up to 1.0 g/kg) for better satiety and metabolic health.

### Recreational Exercisers (Moderate Activity)

**Recommendation: 1.0-1.4 g/kg/day**

If you exercise 2-4 times per week at moderate intensity (brisk walking, cycling, group fitness classes), your protein needs are slightly elevated to support muscle repair and recovery. The ACSM and the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics jointly recommend 1.2-1.4 g/kg/day for this population.

### Strength Training and Muscle Gain

**Recommendation: 1.6-2.2 g/kg/day**

If your goal is to build muscle (hypertrophy), protein becomes especially important. A landmark meta-analysis by Morton et al. (2018), published in the *British Journal of Sports Medicine*, found that protein supplementation beyond 1.6 g/kg/day did not provide additional muscle-building benefits for most people during resistance training. However, intakes up to 2.2 g/kg/day are commonly recommended and well-tolerated.

For a 75 kg (165 lb) person, this translates to roughly 120-165 grams of protein per day.

### Endurance Athletes

**Recommendation: 1.2-1.6 g/kg/day**

Runners, cyclists, swimmers, and other endurance athletes break down muscle tissue during prolonged exercise and need adequate protein for repair. The International Society of Sports Nutrition (ISSN) recommends 1.4-2.0 g/kg/day for athletes in general, with the lower end being appropriate for most endurance-focused individuals.

### Weight Loss and Fat Loss

**Recommendation: 1.2-1.6 g/kg/day (some evidence supports up to 2.0 g/kg)**

When you are eating in a calorie deficit, your body is more likely to break down muscle tissue for energy. Higher protein intake helps preserve lean mass while you lose fat. A study published in *The American Journal of Clinical Nutrition* found that participants who consumed 2.4 g/kg/day during a calorie deficit preserved more lean mass and lost more fat than those eating 1.2 g/kg/day, though both groups improved.

Higher protein also increases satiety, which makes it easier to stick to a reduced-calorie diet.

### Older Adults (65+)

**Recommendation: 1.0-1.2 g/kg/day (or higher if active)**

Aging is associated with sarcopenia -- the progressive loss of muscle mass and strength that accelerates after age 60. The PROT-AGE study group, an international panel of geriatric and nutrition experts, recommends that older adults consume at least 1.0-1.2 g/kg/day to slow muscle loss. Active older adults or those recovering from illness may need up to 1.5 g/kg/day.

### Pregnancy and Lactation

**Recommendation: 1.1-1.3 g/kg/day**

The RDA increases during pregnancy (1.1 g/kg/day) and lactation, reflecting the higher demands of fetal growth and milk production. Some experts recommend even higher intakes (up to 1.5 g/kg/day) during the third trimester. Discuss your individual needs with your healthcare provider.

## How to Distribute Protein Throughout the Day

Research suggests that spreading protein intake evenly across meals is more effective for muscle protein synthesis than consuming most of it in a single meal. A study in the *Journal of Nutrition* found that distributing protein equally across three meals stimulated 24-hour muscle protein synthesis 25% more than eating the majority at dinner (a pattern common in Western diets).

**Practical target:** Aim for 20-40 grams of protein at each meal and 10-20 grams at snacks if needed.

**Example day (120 g total):**

- **Breakfast:** 2 eggs + Greek yogurt with berries = ~30 g
- **Lunch:** Chicken breast salad with chickpeas = ~35 g
- **Snack:** Cottage cheese with almonds = ~20 g
- **Dinner:** Salmon with quinoa and vegetables = ~35 g

## Common Protein Myths Debunked

### Myth 1: "High protein intake damages your kidneys."

**Reality:** For people with healthy kidneys, there is no evidence that high protein intake (up to 2.0-2.5 g/kg/day) causes kidney damage. A comprehensive review published in the *Journal of the International Society of Sports Nutrition* concluded that higher protein diets are safe for healthy individuals. However, people with pre-existing chronic kidney disease should follow their doctor's guidance, as protein restriction may be necessary.

### Myth 2: "Your body can only absorb 30 grams of protein per meal."

**Reality:** Your body can digest and absorb far more than 30 grams per meal. This myth likely stems from early research on muscle protein synthesis, which showed diminishing returns above approximately 20-40 grams per meal in terms of immediate muscle-building stimulation. However, the additional protein is still absorbed and used for other bodily functions. You are not "wasting" protein by eating more.

### Myth 3: "You need a protein shake immediately after your workout."

**Reality:** The "anabolic window" is much wider than the supplement industry suggests. Research from Schoenfeld and Aragon (2018) in the *Journal of the International Society of Sports Nutrition* found that total daily protein intake matters far more than precise nutrient timing. Eating a protein-rich meal within 2-3 hours of your workout is sufficient for most people.

### Myth 4: "Plant protein is inferior to animal protein."

**Reality:** While individual plant proteins may lack one or more essential amino acids, eating a variety of plant-based protein sources throughout the day easily provides a complete amino acid profile. Soy, quinoa, and buckwheat are complete proteins on their own. The Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics confirms that well-planned plant-based diets can meet all protein needs.

### Myth 5: "Older adults need less protein."

**Reality:** The opposite is true. Older adults are less efficient at using dietary protein for muscle synthesis (a phenomenon called "anabolic resistance"), which is why experts recommend they eat more, not less, protein than younger adults.

## Practical Tips for Meeting Your Protein Goals

1. **Audit your current intake** for a few days using a free app or food diary. Most people are surprised by how little or how much they are already eating.
2. **Prioritize whole-food sources** before turning to supplements. Real food provides additional nutrients (fiber, vitamins, minerals) that shakes do not.
3. **Prep protein in advance.** Batch-cook chicken, hard-boil eggs, or prepare a pot of lentils at the start of the week.
4. **Add protein to every meal and snack.** A simple swap like choosing Greek yogurt over regular yogurt can add 10+ grams.
5. **Do not fear variety.** Mix animal and plant sources to get a broad range of amino acids and micronutrients.
