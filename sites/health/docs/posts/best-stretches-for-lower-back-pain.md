# Best Stretches for Lower Back Pain: 8 Gentle Moves for Relief

Lower back pain is one of the most common health complaints worldwide. According to the World Health Organization, low back pain affects roughly 619 million people globally and is a leading cause of disability. The good news is that for many people, a consistent stretching routine can significantly reduce discomfort and improve mobility.

Research published in the *Annals of Internal Medicine* has shown that regular stretching is as effective as yoga for improving back function and reducing pain. Below, you will find eight evidence-based stretches with clear instructions so you can start feeling better today.

Hold each stretch for 20-30 seconds, do 2-3 sets, and aim for a daily practice. A sample 10-minute routine is included at the end. ## Why Stretching Helps Lower Back Pain

The lower back (lumbar spine) supports much of your upper body weight and is involved in nearly every movement you make. Tight hip flexors, hamstrings, and glutes can pull on the pelvis and increase stress on the lumbar region. Stretching these muscle groups helps restore balance, improve blood flow to the tissues, and reduce muscular tension.

The American College of Sports Medicine (ACSM) recommends flexibility exercises at least two to three days per week, holding each stretch for 15-60 seconds. For back pain specifically, daily stretching tends to produce the best results.

## Before You Begin: Important Safety Notes

- **Stop immediately** if any stretch causes sharp, shooting, or radiating pain.
- **Breathe deeply** throughout each stretch. Never hold your breath.
- **Warm up first** with 2-3 minutes of gentle walking or marching in place.
- **Move slowly** into each position. Stretching should feel like mild tension, not pain.

## The 8 Best Stretches for Lower Back Pain

### 1. Child's Pose (Balasana)

**Targets:** Lower back, lats, hips

**How to do it:**

1. Kneel on the floor with your knees hip-width apart and your big toes touching.
2. Sit your hips back toward your heels.
3. Walk your hands forward along the floor, lowering your chest toward the ground.
4. Let your forehead rest on the floor or a pillow.
5. Relax your arms alongside your body or keep them extended in front of you.

**Hold:** 20-30 seconds | **Sets:** 2-3

**When to do it:** Anytime, especially first thing in the morning or before bed.

**Who should avoid it:** People with knee injuries or severe hip limitations should modify by placing a pillow between the thighs and calves.

### 2. Cat-Cow Stretch

**Targets:** Entire spine, abdominals, back extensors

**How to do it:**

1. Start on all fours with your wrists under your shoulders and knees under your hips.
2. **Cow:** Inhale, drop your belly toward the floor, lift your chest and tailbone, and look slightly upward.
3. **Cat:** Exhale, round your spine toward the ceiling, tuck your tailbone, and bring your chin to your chest.
4. Flow smoothly between the two positions with your breath.

**Hold:** 3-5 seconds per position | **Repetitions:** 8-10 cycles | **Sets:** 2

**When to do it:** Morning or as a warm-up before exercise.

**Who should avoid it:** Those with wrist pain can perform this on their forearms instead.

### 3. Knee-to-Chest Stretch

**Targets:** Lower back, glutes, hip flexors

**How to do it:**

1. Lie on your back with both knees bent and feet flat on the floor.
2. Bring one knee toward your chest, clasping your hands just below the kneecap.
3. Gently pull the knee closer until you feel a comfortable stretch in your lower back.
4. Keep your opposite foot flat on the floor and your lower back pressed gently into the mat.
5. Repeat on the other side.

**Hold:** 20-30 seconds per side | **Sets:** 2-3

**When to do it:** Anytime -- especially helpful after long periods of sitting.

**Who should avoid it:** Those with hip replacements should consult their surgeon before performing this stretch.

### 4. Piriformis Stretch (Figure-Four Stretch)

**Targets:** Piriformis, glutes, outer hip

**How to do it:**

1. Lie on your back with both knees bent.
2. Cross your right ankle over your left knee, forming a "figure four."
3. Reach both hands behind your left thigh and gently pull your left leg toward your chest.
4. Keep your head and shoulders relaxed on the floor.
5. Repeat on the other side.

**Hold:** 20-30 seconds per side | **Sets:** 2-3

**When to do it:** After sitting for extended periods or post-workout.

**Who should avoid it:** If you feel pinching in the front of the hip, reduce the range of motion or skip this stretch.

### 5. Pelvic Tilts

**Targets:** Lower back, deep abdominals

**How to do it:**

1. Lie on your back with knees bent and feet flat, arms at your sides.
2. Flatten your lower back against the floor by gently tightening your abdominal muscles and tilting your pelvis upward.
3. Hold the contraction briefly, then release back to a neutral position.

**Hold:** 5-10 seconds | **Repetitions:** 10-15 | **Sets:** 2

**When to do it:** As a warm-up or standalone exercise throughout the day.

**Who should avoid it:** This is generally safe for most people. If you experience pain, reduce the range of motion.

### 6. Seated Spinal Twist

**Targets:** Lower and mid-back, obliques, hips

**How to do it:**

1. Sit on the floor with both legs extended in front of you.
2. Bend your right knee and cross your right foot over your left leg, placing it flat on the floor near your left knee.
3. Place your right hand behind you for support.
4. Bring your left elbow to the outside of your right knee and gently twist to the right.
5. Keep your spine tall -- think about lengthening upward as you rotate.
6. Repeat on the other side.

**Hold:** 20-30 seconds per side | **Sets:** 2

**When to do it:** Midday or after prolonged sitting.

**Who should avoid it:** People with herniated discs should avoid spinal rotation unless cleared by a healthcare provider.

### 7. Sphinx Pose

**Targets:** Lower back (gentle extension), abdominals

**How to do it:**

1. Lie face down with your legs extended behind you.
2. Place your forearms on the floor with elbows directly under your shoulders.
3. Press your forearms into the floor and gently lift your chest.
4. Keep your hips and legs relaxed on the ground.
5. Look straight ahead and breathe deeply.

**Hold:** 20-30 seconds | **Sets:** 2-3

**When to do it:** Morning or after a long day of sitting hunched forward.

**Who should avoid it:** Those with spinal stenosis or pain that worsens with back extension should skip this stretch.

### 8. Standing Hamstring Stretch

**Targets:** Hamstrings, lower back

**How to do it:**

1. Stand and place one heel on a low step, bench, or sturdy surface (6-12 inches high).
2. Keep the raised leg straight but do not lock the knee.
3. Hinge forward at the hips -- not the waist -- until you feel a stretch along the back of your thigh.
4. Keep your back flat and chest lifted.
5. Repeat on the other side.

**Hold:** 20-30 seconds per side | **Sets:** 2-3

**When to do it:** After a warm-up or post-exercise cooldown.

**Who should avoid it:** If you have sciatica that worsens with forward bending, try bending the knee slightly or skip this stretch.

## Sample 10-Minute Lower Back Stretch Routine

This routine flows from floor-based to standing stretches. Perform it daily for the best results.

| Order | Stretch                   | Duration              |
|-------|---------------------------|-----------------------|
| 1     | Pelvic Tilts              | 10 reps (about 1 min) |
| 2     | Cat-Cow                   | 8 cycles (about 1.5 min) |
| 3     | Child's Pose              | 30 sec x 2 (1 min)   |
| 4     | Knee-to-Chest (each side) | 20 sec x 2 (1.5 min) |
| 5     | Piriformis Stretch (each side) | 20 sec x 2 (1.5 min) |
| 6     | Seated Spinal Twist (each side) | 20 sec x 2 (1.5 min) |
| 7     | Sphinx Pose               | 20 sec x 2 (1 min)   |
| 8     | Standing Hamstring Stretch (each side) | 20 sec x 2 (1.5 min) |

**Total time:** Approximately 10-11 minutes.

## Tips for Long-Term Back Health

- **Be consistent.** Research from the NIH National Institute of Neurological Disorders and Stroke shows that regular physical activity and stretching are among the most effective strategies for managing chronic low back pain.
- **Strengthen your core.** Stretching alone is not enough. Pair these stretches with core-strengthening exercises like planks and bridges for better spinal support.
- **Mind your posture.** Sit with your feet flat on the floor, back supported, and screen at eye level when working at a desk.
- **Stay active.** Walking 20-30 minutes a day can help keep your back healthy and reduce stiffness.

## When to See a Doctor

Stretching is helpful for many types of lower back pain, but it is not a cure-all. Seek medical attention if you experience:

- Pain that lasts more than 4-6 weeks without improvement
- Numbness or tingling in the legs
- Pain after a fall, accident, or injury
- Loss of bladder or bowel control (seek emergency care)
- Unexplained weight loss alongside back pain
