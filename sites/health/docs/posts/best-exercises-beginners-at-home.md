# 10 Best Exercises for Beginners at Home (No Equipment Needed)

Starting an exercise routine can feel overwhelming, especially if you have never set foot in a gym or do not own any equipment. The good news is that your body is the only tool you need. Bodyweight exercises are effective, scalable, and backed by exercise science as a legitimate way to build strength, endurance, and flexibility.

The World Health Organization recommends that adults aged 18-64 get at least 150-300 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic activity per week, along with muscle-strengthening activities on 2 or more days per week. The exercises in this guide will help you meet those strength guidelines right from your living room.

**TL;DR:** The 10 best beginner exercises at home are: bodyweight squats, push-ups (or knee push-ups), glute bridges, lunges, planks, dead bugs, wall sit, bird dogs, incline push-ups, and high knees. Each exercise is described with proper form, targeted muscles, and beginner-friendly reps and sets. A complete 20-minute workout plan combining these exercises is included at the end.

## Why Bodyweight Training Works

You might wonder whether exercising without weights can really make a difference. The research says yes. A systematic review published in *Sports Medicine* found that bodyweight training produces significant improvements in muscular strength and endurance, particularly in untrained individuals. For beginners, the resistance provided by your own body is more than enough to stimulate muscle growth and cardiovascular adaptation.

Additional benefits include:

- **Zero cost.** No gym membership or equipment required.
- **Low barrier to entry.** You can start in your bedroom, living room, or backyard.
- **Reduced injury risk.** Bodyweight exercises allow you to learn movement patterns without external load.
- **Easy to scale.** Every exercise can be made easier or harder by adjusting range of motion, tempo, or adding pauses.

## Before You Start

- **Clear a small space.** You need roughly 6 feet by 6 feet of open floor.
- **Warm up for 3-5 minutes.** March in place, do arm circles, or perform light jumping jacks to raise your heart rate and prepare your joints.
- **Focus on form over speed.** Doing 8 reps with perfect form is far more valuable than rushing through 20 sloppy ones.
- **Breathe.** Exhale during the effort phase (pushing, pulling, or lifting) and inhale during the return phase.

## The 10 Best Exercises for Beginners

### 1. Bodyweight Squats

**Muscles targeted:** Quadriceps, glutes, hamstrings, core

**How to do it:**

1. Stand with feet slightly wider than hip-width apart, toes turned out about 15-30 degrees.
2. Brace your core and keep your chest lifted.
3. Push your hips back and bend your knees as if you are sitting into a chair.
4. Lower until your thighs are parallel to the floor (or as low as comfortable).
5. Press through your heels to stand back up.

**Common mistakes to avoid:** Letting your knees cave inward, rising onto your toes, or rounding your lower back.

**Beginner prescription:** 3 sets of 10-12 reps. Rest 45-60 seconds between sets.

### 2. Push-Ups (or Knee Push-Ups)

**Muscles targeted:** Chest, shoulders (anterior deltoids), triceps, core

**How to do it (standard):**

1. Start in a high plank position with hands slightly wider than shoulder-width, fingers pointing forward.
2. Your body should form a straight line from head to heels.
3. Lower your chest toward the floor by bending your elbows to about 90 degrees.
4. Push back up to the starting position.

**Modification (knee push-ups):** Drop your knees to the floor, keeping your hips extended so your body forms a straight line from head to knees. This reduces the load by about 50%, making it an excellent starting point.

**Beginner prescription:** 3 sets of 5-10 reps (knee or standard). Rest 60 seconds between sets.

### 3. Glute Bridges

**Muscles targeted:** Glutes, hamstrings, core, lower back

**How to do it:**

1. Lie on your back with knees bent and feet flat on the floor, hip-width apart.
2. Arms rest at your sides with palms down.
3. Press through your heels and squeeze your glutes to lift your hips until your body forms a straight line from shoulders to knees.
4. Hold the top position for 1-2 seconds, squeezing your glutes.
5. Lower slowly back down.

**Common mistakes to avoid:** Overarching your lower back at the top or pushing through your toes instead of your heels.

**Beginner prescription:** 3 sets of 12-15 reps. Rest 45 seconds between sets.

### 4. Reverse Lunges

**Muscles targeted:** Quadriceps, glutes, hamstrings, calves, core (balance)

**How to do it:**

1. Stand with feet hip-width apart, hands on your hips or at your sides.
2. Step your right foot back about 2-3 feet.
3. Lower your body until both knees form approximately 90-degree angles.
4. Your front knee should stay directly above your ankle.
5. Push through your front heel to return to standing.
6. Alternate legs each rep.

**Why reverse instead of forward lunges:** Reverse lunges place less stress on the front knee, making them more beginner-friendly and easier to balance.

**Beginner prescription:** 3 sets of 8-10 reps per leg. Rest 60 seconds between sets.

### 5. Plank (Forearm or High Plank)

**Muscles targeted:** Core (rectus abdominis, transverse abdominis, obliques), shoulders, glutes

**How to do it:**

1. Place your forearms on the floor with elbows directly under your shoulders.
2. Extend your legs behind you, tucking your toes under.
3. Engage your core by imagining you are bracing for a gentle push.
4. Your body should form a straight line from head to heels.
5. Hold the position without letting your hips sag or pike upward.

**Modification:** Perform on your knees if a full plank is too challenging at first.

**Beginner prescription:** 3 holds of 15-30 seconds. Rest 45 seconds between holds. Add 5 seconds each week.

### 6. Dead Bugs

**Muscles targeted:** Deep core muscles (transverse abdominis), hip flexors

**How to do it:**

1. Lie on your back with arms extended straight toward the ceiling and knees bent at 90 degrees (shins parallel to the floor).
2. Press your lower back firmly into the floor -- this is your anchor point.
3. Slowly extend your right arm overhead and your left leg toward the floor simultaneously.
4. Go only as far as you can while keeping your lower back pressed into the floor.
5. Return to the starting position and repeat on the other side.

**Why this exercise matters:** Dead bugs teach core stability in a safe, low-impact way. They are frequently recommended by physical therapists for people with back pain, as noted in clinical guidelines from the ACSM.

**Beginner prescription:** 3 sets of 8-10 reps per side. Rest 45 seconds between sets.

### 7. Wall Sit

**Muscles targeted:** Quadriceps, glutes, calves, core (isometric)

**How to do it:**

1. Stand with your back flat against a wall.
2. Slide down until your thighs are parallel to the floor (or slightly above parallel for beginners).
3. Keep your knees directly above your ankles, not pushed past your toes.
4. Press your entire back flat against the wall.
5. Hold the position, breathing steadily.

**Beginner prescription:** 3 holds of 15-30 seconds. Rest 60 seconds between holds.

### 8. Bird Dogs

**Muscles targeted:** Lower back (erector spinae), glutes, core, shoulders

**How to do it:**

1. Start on all fours with wrists under shoulders and knees under hips.
2. Brace your core and keep your back flat (imagine balancing a glass of water on your lower back).
3. Slowly extend your right arm forward and your left leg back simultaneously.
4. Hold for 2-3 seconds at full extension.
5. Return to the starting position and switch sides.

**Common mistakes to avoid:** Rotating your hips or arching your back. Move slowly and focus on stability.

**Beginner prescription:** 3 sets of 8-10 reps per side. Rest 45 seconds between sets.

### 9. Incline Push-Ups

**Muscles targeted:** Chest (lower emphasis), shoulders, triceps, core

**How to do it:**

1. Place your hands on a sturdy elevated surface -- a countertop, bench, or the back of a couch.
2. Walk your feet back until your body forms a straight line from head to heels.
3. Lower your chest toward the surface by bending your elbows.
4. Push back up to the starting position.

**Why this is valuable:** Incline push-ups bridge the gap between knee push-ups and full push-ups. As you get stronger, gradually lower the surface height until you can perform push-ups on the floor.

**Beginner prescription:** 3 sets of 8-12 reps. Rest 60 seconds between sets.

### 10. High Knees (Cardio)

**Muscles targeted:** Hip flexors, quadriceps, calves, core; cardiovascular system

**How to do it:**

1. Stand with feet hip-width apart.
2. Drive your right knee toward your chest as high as comfortable.
3. Quickly switch legs, driving your left knee up as the right foot returns to the floor.
4. Pump your arms in sync with your legs (opposite arm, opposite leg).
5. Maintain an upright posture -- do not lean back.

**Modification:** March in place with high knees at a slower pace if the jumping version is too intense.

**Beginner prescription:** 3 sets of 20-30 seconds. Rest 60 seconds between sets.

## Sample 20-Minute Beginner Workout Plan

This workout combines the exercises above into a structured session. Warm up for 3-5 minutes first (marching in place, arm circles, and leg swings).

### Circuit A (Repeat 2 times)

| Exercise            | Reps / Duration     | Rest         |
|---------------------|---------------------|--------------|
| Bodyweight Squats   | 12 reps             | 30 sec       |
| Knee Push-Ups       | 8 reps              | 30 sec       |
| Glute Bridges       | 12 reps             | 30 sec       |
| Dead Bugs           | 8 reps per side     | 30 sec       |
| High Knees          | 20 seconds          | 60 sec (then repeat circuit) |

### Circuit B (Repeat 2 times)

| Exercise            | Reps / Duration     | Rest         |
|---------------------|---------------------|--------------|
| Reverse Lunges      | 8 reps per leg      | 30 sec       |
| Incline Push-Ups    | 10 reps             | 30 sec       |
| Bird Dogs           | 8 reps per side     | 30 sec       |
| Wall Sit            | 20 seconds          | 30 sec       |
| Plank               | 20 seconds          | 60 sec (then repeat circuit) |

**Cooldown:** 3-5 minutes of light stretching focusing on the legs, chest, and hips.

**Total time:** Approximately 20-22 minutes including warm-up and cooldown.

## How to Progress

The ACSM's guidelines for resistance training progression recommend increasing volume (reps, sets, or duration) gradually over time:

- **Weeks 1-2:** Follow the plan above as written. Focus on learning proper form.
- **Weeks 3-4:** Add 2-3 reps to each exercise or increase hold times by 5-10 seconds.
- **Weeks 5-6:** Add a third round to each circuit or reduce rest periods by 10-15 seconds.
- **Weeks 7-8:** Begin replacing easier variations with harder ones (knee push-ups to incline push-ups, incline push-ups to full push-ups).
- **Beyond 8 weeks:** Consider adding resistance bands, dumbbells, or more advanced bodyweight progressions (pistol squat variations, decline push-ups, single-leg glute bridges).

## Tips for Staying Consistent

- **Schedule your workouts** like appointments. The same time each day builds a habit faster.
- **Start with 2-3 sessions per week.** You can build up to 4-5 sessions as your fitness and confidence grow.
- **Track your progress.** Write down your reps, sets, and how you felt. Seeing improvement over time is one of the best motivators.
- **Do not skip the warm-up.** It reduces injury risk and prepares your nervous system for work.
- **Celebrate small wins.** Your first full push-up, an extra 10 seconds on the plank, or just showing up on a day you did not feel like it -- these all count.
