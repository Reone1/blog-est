# Bodyweight Exercises for a Full Body Workout: The Complete Guide

You do not need a gym membership, a barbell, or a single piece of equipment to build a stronger, fitter body. Bodyweight training — also called calisthenics — uses your own mass as resistance, and the science shows it works. From improving muscular strength and endurance to boosting cardiovascular fitness and body composition, a well-designed full-body bodyweight routine can deliver results comparable to traditional gym training.

This guide gives you everything: the best exercises, how to do them with proper form, muscle groups targeted, a ready-to-use weekly plan, and advice on progressing over time.

<div class="tldr">

**TL;DR:** A full body bodyweight workout targets legs, glutes, core, chest, back, and shoulders using moves like squats, push-ups, hip hinges, rows, and planks. Train each muscle group at least twice a week, focus on progressive overload (more reps, slower tempo, harder variations), and you will gain real strength — no gym required.

## Does Bodyweight Training Actually Work? {#does-bodyweight-training-actually-work}

A common concern is whether training without external weights produces real strength gains. The evidence says yes — especially when you apply progressive overload.

A 2018 study by Kotarsky et al. published in the *Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research* (PMID: 29466268) found that progressive calisthenic push-up training produced comparable increases in upper-body muscular strength and thickness to traditional bench press training over four weeks. The key word is *progressive* — exercises must become progressively harder as you adapt.

A 2021 systematic review and network meta-analysis published in *Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise* (Lopez et al., PMID: 33433148) confirmed that hypertrophy — muscle growth — is largely **load-independent**. Lower-load training, including bodyweight work, produces muscle growth similar to high-load training when sets are taken close to muscular failure. This is a critical finding: it means bodyweight exercises can build muscle as long as the effort level is high enough.

For cardiovascular fitness and body composition, a 2017 RCT by Sperlich et al. published in *Frontiers in Physiology* (PMID: 28420999) found that a 9-week functional high-intensity circuit training (HICT) program — all bodyweight-based — significantly improved peak oxygen uptake, lean mass, and upper- and lower-limb strength in overweight women.

**Bottom line:** Bodyweight training builds strength, muscle, and fitness. The requirement is progressive challenge, consistent effort, and training each muscle group at least twice per week.

## Full Body vs. Split Routines: What's Better for Beginners? {#full-body-vs-split-routines}

If you are new to exercise or returning after a break, full body workouts are the recommended starting point.

- **Full body workouts** train every major muscle group in each session. You hit each group 2–3 times per week, which research consistently identifies as optimal for hypertrophy and strength development (Schoenfeld et al., 2016, *Sports Medicine*, PMID: 27102172).
- **Split routines** (e.g., chest/back day, leg day) train fewer muscle groups per session but with higher per-session volume. These are better suited to intermediate and advanced trainees who need more per-muscle volume than a single full-body session can provide.

For a beginner doing 2–4 sessions per week, a full body approach covers all muscle groups with the right training frequency without requiring daily workouts.

## The 10 Best Bodyweight Exercises for a Full Body Workout {#the-10-best-exercises}

The exercises below collectively target every major muscle group: legs, glutes, hamstrings, chest, back, shoulders, and core. Each exercise includes a difficulty rating, muscles targeted, step-by-step instructions, and progression options.

---

### 1. Bodyweight Squat

**Difficulty:** Beginner | **Muscles:** Quadriceps, glutes, hamstrings, core

**How to do it:**
1. Stand with feet shoulder-width apart, toes turned out slightly (15–30°).
2. Brace your core and keep your chest tall.
3. Push your hips back and bend your knees, lowering until your thighs are parallel to the floor (or as low as comfortable without your heels rising).
4. Drive through your heels to return to standing. Squeeze your glutes at the top.

**Common mistakes:** Knees caving inward, heels lifting, excessive forward lean.

**Beginner prescription:** 3 sets × 12–15 reps | Rest: 60 sec

**Progressions:** Jump squat → Bulgarian split squat → Single-leg squat (pistol)

---

### 2. Push-Up

**Difficulty:** Beginner–Intermediate | **Muscles:** Chest, anterior deltoids, triceps, core

**How to do it:**
1. Start in a high plank: hands slightly wider than shoulder-width, body forming a straight line from head to heels.
2. Lower your chest toward the floor by bending your elbows at roughly a 45-degree angle to your torso (not flared out wide).
3. Push back up explosively, fully extending your elbows at the top.

**Modification:** Elevate your hands on a countertop or bench to reduce the load. As you get stronger, gradually lower the surface height.

**Beginner prescription:** 3 sets × 8–12 reps | Rest: 60 sec

**Progressions:** Decline push-up (feet elevated) → Archer push-up → One-arm push-up negatives

---

### 3. Glute Bridge

**Difficulty:** Beginner | **Muscles:** Glutes, hamstrings, lower back, core

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on your back with knees bent to 90°, feet flat on the floor hip-width apart, arms at your sides.
2. Press through your heels and squeeze your glutes to drive your hips upward until your body forms a straight line from shoulders to knees.
3. Hold the top position for 1–2 seconds, squeezing hard.
4. Lower slowly and repeat.

**Beginner prescription:** 3 sets × 15 reps | Rest: 45 sec

**Progressions:** Single-leg glute bridge → Hip thrust with feet elevated → Banded glute bridge

---

### 4. Reverse Lunge

**Difficulty:** Beginner | **Muscles:** Quadriceps, glutes, hamstrings, calves, core (balance)

**How to do it:**
1. Stand tall with feet hip-width apart.
2. Step one foot back about 2–3 feet and lower your rear knee toward the floor (without touching it).
3. Both knees should form approximately 90-degree angles at the bottom. Your front knee stays above your ankle — not pushed past your toes.
4. Push through your front heel to return to standing. Alternate legs.

**Why reverse over forward lunges:** Reverse lunges reduce shear stress on the front knee and are easier to control for beginners.

**Beginner prescription:** 3 sets × 10 reps per leg | Rest: 60 sec

**Progressions:** Walking lunge → Deficit reverse lunge (front foot elevated) → Bulgarian split squat

---

### 5. Hip Hinge (Bodyweight Romanian Deadlift)

**Difficulty:** Beginner | **Muscles:** Hamstrings, glutes, lower back (spinal erectors)

**How to do it:**
1. Stand with feet hip-width apart, a slight bend in your knees.
2. Push your hips back (think: close a car door with your hips) while maintaining a flat back and neutral spine.
3. Lower your torso until you feel a stretch in your hamstrings — typically until your torso is about parallel to the floor.
4. Drive your hips forward and squeeze your glutes to stand back up.

**Why this matters:** Most people are "quad dominant" and underdevelop their posterior chain (hamstrings, glutes). This movement corrects that imbalance and is foundational for back health.

**Beginner prescription:** 3 sets × 12 reps | Rest: 45 sec

**Progressions:** Single-leg RDL (bodyweight) → Nordic hamstring curl → Single-leg RDL with load

---

### 6. Inverted Row (Table Row)

**Difficulty:** Beginner | **Muscles:** Upper back (rhomboids, mid-trapezius), biceps, rear deltoids

**How to do it:**
1. Lie underneath a sturdy table or use a low bar. Grip the edge of the table (or bar) with an overhand grip, hands about shoulder-width.
2. Keep your body rigid and straight from head to heels.
3. Pull your chest up toward the table edge, squeezing your shoulder blades together at the top.
4. Lower slowly back to the starting position.

**Why rows matter in a full body routine:** Most beginners over-prioritize pushing (push-ups) and neglect pulling. Rows balance this out, reducing the risk of shoulder impingement and poor posture.

**Modification:** Bend your knees and place feet flat on the floor to reduce the load.

**Beginner prescription:** 3 sets × 8–10 reps | Rest: 60 sec

**Progressions:** Body position more horizontal → Pull-up negatives → Full pull-up

---

### 7. Plank

**Difficulty:** Beginner | **Muscles:** Deep core (transverse abdominis, obliques), shoulders, glutes (isometric)

**How to do it:**
1. Place forearms on the floor with elbows directly under your shoulders.
2. Extend your legs behind you, toes tucked under.
3. Engage your core: imagine bracing as if someone is about to poke your stomach. Keep your hips level — do not let them sag or pike up.
4. Hold and breathe steadily.

**Beginner prescription:** 3 holds × 20–30 sec | Rest: 45 sec | Add 5 sec per week.

**Progressions:** High plank → Plank shoulder taps → RKC plank → Side plank → Plank with leg raise

---

### 8. Dead Bug

**Difficulty:** Beginner | **Muscles:** Deep core (transverse abdominis, hip flexors), stability

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on your back, arms extended toward the ceiling, knees bent at 90° (shins parallel to floor — "tabletop" position).
2. Press your lower back firmly into the floor. This lumbar contact point must not lift throughout the exercise.
3. Slowly extend your right arm overhead and your left leg toward the floor simultaneously, going only as far as your lower back stays pressed down.
4. Return to the starting position, then repeat on the other side (left arm + right leg).

**Why this exercise:** Dead bugs train spinal stability under load — a key predictor of lower back health. They are frequently recommended in clinical physical therapy settings for core rehabilitation.

**Beginner prescription:** 3 sets × 8 reps per side | Rest: 45 sec

---

### 9. Superman Hold

**Difficulty:** Beginner | **Muscles:** Lower back (erector spinae), glutes, rear deltoids (posterior chain)

**How to do it:**
1. Lie face down on the floor, arms extended overhead, legs straight.
2. Simultaneously lift your arms, chest, and legs off the floor, squeezing your glutes and upper back.
3. Hold the top position for 2–3 seconds.
4. Lower slowly and repeat.

**Beginner prescription:** 3 sets × 10–12 reps | Rest: 45 sec

**Progressions:** Single-arm/single-leg Superman → Back extension on a bench

---

### 10. Mountain Climbers

**Difficulty:** Beginner–Intermediate | **Muscles:** Core, hip flexors, shoulders, cardiovascular system

**How to do it:**
1. Start in a high plank position with your wrists directly under your shoulders.
2. Drive your right knee toward your chest, then quickly switch and drive your left knee in as the right foot returns to the floor.
3. Alternate legs at a controlled pace. Keep your hips level — do not bounce them up with each rep.

**Pacing tip:** For a strength-endurance focus, move at a moderate, controlled pace. For a cardiovascular challenge, increase speed.

**Beginner prescription:** 3 sets × 20–30 sec | Rest: 60 sec

## Sample Full Body Bodyweight Workout Plans {#sample-workout-plans}

### Plan A — 3 Days Per Week (Beginner)

Perform on non-consecutive days (e.g., Monday / Wednesday / Friday). Warm up for 3–5 minutes first: march in place, arm circles, leg swings, and hip rotations.

**Workout (same each session, all 3 days)**

| Exercise | Sets × Reps / Duration | Rest |
|---|---|---|
| Bodyweight Squat | 3 × 12 | 60 sec |
| Push-Up | 3 × 8–10 | 60 sec |
| Glute Bridge | 3 × 15 | 45 sec |
| Reverse Lunge | 3 × 10 per leg | 60 sec |
| Inverted Row (table) | 3 × 8 | 60 sec |
| Plank | 3 × 25 sec | 45 sec |
| Dead Bug | 3 × 8 per side | 45 sec |
| Superman Hold | 3 × 10 | 45 sec |

**Total time:** ~35–40 minutes including warm-up and cooldown.

**Cooldown:** 5 minutes of light stretching — hip flexors, hamstrings, chest, and thoracic spine.

---

### Plan B — 4 Days Per Week (Upper / Lower Split)

Once you can comfortably complete Plan A for 4 consecutive weeks, consider splitting into upper and lower sessions to allow more volume per muscle group.

**Day A (Upper Body Focus)**

| Exercise | Sets × Reps | Rest |
|---|---|---|
| Push-Up | 4 × 10–12 | 60 sec |
| Inverted Row | 4 × 8–10 | 60 sec |
| Pike Push-Up (shoulder focus) | 3 × 8 | 60 sec |
| Dead Bug | 3 × 10 per side | 45 sec |
| Plank | 3 × 30 sec | 45 sec |
| Mountain Climbers | 3 × 30 sec | 60 sec |

**Day B (Lower Body Focus)**

| Exercise | Sets × Reps | Rest |
|---|---|---|
| Bodyweight Squat | 4 × 15 | 60 sec |
| Reverse Lunge | 3 × 12 per leg | 60 sec |
| Glute Bridge | 4 × 15 | 45 sec |
| Hip Hinge (RDL) | 3 × 12 | 45 sec |
| Superman Hold | 3 × 12 | 45 sec |
| Jump Squat (optional) | 3 × 10 | 60 sec |

**Weekly schedule:** Day A → Day B → Rest → Day A → Day B → Rest → Rest (or active recovery).

## How to Progress Over Time {#how-to-progress}

Progressive overload — gradually increasing the challenge — is the fundamental mechanism behind strength and muscle gains. Without it, your body adapts and results plateau. Here are the main tools for progressing bodyweight exercises:

**1. Add reps or sets.** The simplest form of progression. Once you can do 3 sets of 12 with good form, add 2 reps per set until you reach 3 × 15, then add a fourth set.

**2. Slow the tempo.** A push-up lowered over 4 seconds (4-1-1 tempo: 4 seconds down, 1 second pause, 1 second up) dramatically increases time under tension and difficulty without adding load.

**3. Reduce rest periods.** Shortening rest from 60 seconds to 45 or 30 seconds increases cardiovascular demand and metabolic stress.

**4. Advance to harder exercise variations.** This is the most powerful tool in bodyweight training. A progression ladder might look like:
- **Push-up:** Incline push-up → Knee push-up → Standard push-up → Decline push-up → Archer push-up → One-arm push-up
- **Squat:** Bodyweight squat → Jump squat → Bulgarian split squat → Pistol squat (single-leg)
- **Core:** Plank → Plank shoulder taps → RKC plank → Hollow body hold

**5. Increase training frequency.** A 2016 meta-analysis by Schoenfeld et al. (*Sports Medicine*, PMID: 27102172) found that training a muscle group twice per week produces significantly greater hypertrophy than training it once per week. Moving from 2 to 3 full-body sessions per week is a meaningful progression.

**Weeks 1–2:** Learn the exercises. Focus on form over reps.
**Weeks 3–4:** Add 1–2 reps to each set.
**Weeks 5–6:** Reduce rest by 10–15 seconds or slow the tempo.
**Weeks 7–8:** Replace one easier variation with a harder one.
**Beyond 8 weeks:** Consider adding resistance (backpack with books, resistance bands) or progressing to more advanced calisthenics skills.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid {#common-mistakes}

**Skipping posterior chain exercises.** Most people default to squats and push-ups and neglect hip hinges and rows. This leads to muscle imbalances, poor posture, and shoulder problems over time. Always include at least one pull and one hip hinge movement per session.

**Training to fatigue but not near failure.** Research on muscle hypertrophy (Lopez et al., 2021, PMID: 33433148) confirms that bodyweight training builds muscle when sets are taken close to muscular failure. If your last 2–3 reps do not feel challenging, the stimulus is too low.

**No warm-up.** Cold muscles and joints are more vulnerable to strain. Even 3–5 minutes of light movement — marching in place, hip circles, arm swings — meaningfully prepares your body.

**Inconsistency.** Two consistent sessions per week outperform sporadic intensive training every time. Consistency over intensity is the principle that determines long-term results.

**Ignoring sleep and nutrition.** Exercise is the stimulus. Recovery is where adaptation happens. Prioritize 7–9 hours of sleep per night and ensure adequate protein intake (around 1.6 g/kg body weight per day) to support muscle repair.
